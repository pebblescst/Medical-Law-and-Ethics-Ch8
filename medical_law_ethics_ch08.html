Chapter focus: Chapter 8 bridges two critical areas — the privacy and security framework protecting patient health information (from constitutional foundations through HIPAA's detailed rules) and the federal fraud and abuse laws that govern financial conduct in the healthcare industry. Both areas carry severe civil and criminal penalties and are major compliance priorities for every healthcare organization.
At a Glance — HIPAA's Four Standards
Standard 1 — Transactions & Code Sets
Establishes uniform electronic data standards for healthcare transactions (claims, remittances, eligibility checks) and medical code sets (ICD, CPT, HCPCS). Ensures different systems can communicate consistently in electronic billing.
Standard 2 — Privacy Rule
Protects the privacy of individually identifiable health information (PHI) — regardless of form. Defines who may access, use, and disclose PHI; establishes patient rights regarding their health information.
Standard 3 — Security Rule
Establishes requirements for protecting the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of ELECTRONIC PHI (ePHI) specifically. Requires technical, administrative, and physical safeguards.
Standard 4 — National Identifier Standards
Establishes unique National Provider Identifiers (NPI) for healthcare providers, payers, and employers used in all electronic transactions — enabling consistent identification across the system.
The Right to Privacy — Constitutional Foundation
Privacy: Freedom from unauthorized intrusion. The right to privacy is not explicitly stated in the U.S. Constitution, but is derived from multiple constitutional amendments and has been recognized by the Supreme Court as a fundamental constitutional right.
Six amendments that support the right to privacy:
1st
Freedom of speech, religion, press, and assembly — protection from government control of personal beliefs and associations; foundational to personal autonomy
3rd
Prohibition on quartering soldiers in private homes — establishes the principle that the home is a private sanctuary protected from government intrusion
4th
Protection from unreasonable searches and seizures — requires warrants based on probable cause; most directly relevant to privacy of persons and property from government intrusion
5th
Protection against self-incrimination and due process — the right to remain silent; protects individuals from being compelled to disclose information against themselves
9th
Rights retained by the people — states that the enumeration of specific rights in the Constitution shall not deny or disparage other rights retained by the people; used by courts to recognize unenumerated rights including privacy
14th
Equal protection and due process — prohibits states from depriving persons of life, liberty, or property without due process of law; used to extend federal constitutional rights (including privacy) to state government actions
Four federal privacy law principles:
Minimum necessary collection
Information collected about individuals should be limited to what is necessary to carry out the functions of the business or government agency collecting it — no collecting "just in case"
Limited internal access
Access to personal information within the organization should be limited to employees who must use it in performing their job duties — "need to know" access principle
No unauthorized external release
Personal information cannot be released outside the organization without permission of the subject — the individual controls who receives their information
Transparency and accuracy
The person should know that information is being collected and should have the opportunity to check the information for accuracy — notice and access rights
Confidentiality & Privileged Communication
Confidentiality
The act of holding information in confidence — not to be released to unauthorized individuals. Healthcare practitioners have both an ethical and legal duty of confidentiality toward their patients. This duty applies to all patient information regardless of how it was obtained or in what form it exists (verbal, written, electronic).
Privileged communication
Information held confidential within a protected relationship. In law, certain relationships are recognized as creating a "privilege" that protects communications from compelled disclosure in legal proceedings — including physician-patient, attorney-client, priest-penitent, and therapist-patient relationships. The patient (not the provider) holds the privilege and can waive or assert it.
Maintaining confidentiality in practice:
✓ Obtain signed consent before releasing any information
✓ Avoid making judgments about patients' morals or lifestyle choices
✓ Treat financial information as confidential — insurance, billing, and payment information is protected PHI
✓ Do not use the patient's name on the phone if others are nearby — "overhearing" is a privacy breach
✓ Use caution when leaving messages on voicemail — leave minimal information; never leave diagnosis or treatment details
✓ Keep patient information out of sight from other patients or visitors — turn screens, cover charts
✓ Make only legal exceptions to disclosure — know what is and is not a permitted disclosure
Waiving confidentiality — three circumstances:
✦ A third party requests a medical examination (e.g., employment physical) and that party pays the physician's fee — the information goes to the paying party, not the patient
✦ A lawsuit results in patient records being subpoenaed — legal process overrides confidentiality
✦ Patient signs a written waiver agreeing to release — explicit patient authorization
HIPAA Standard 2 — The Privacy Rule
The Privacy Rule protects PHI (Protected Health Information) from unauthorized disclosure — regardless of whether it is spoken, written, or in electronic form. It is the broadest and most comprehensive of HIPAA's standards.
What is PHI? — Three-part definition:
Past, present, or future physical or mental health conditions
Any diagnosis, symptom, complaint, test result, or health status — including mental health information; extends to historical health conditions, not just current ones
Documentation of the provision of healthcare
Records of treatment, procedures, prescriptions, clinical notes, and any documentation created during the course of providing healthcare services
Past, present, or future payment for healthcare
Billing records, insurance claims, payment history, and any financial information relating to healthcare — all protected as PHI even though it may appear to be purely financial data
PHI must be protected in all three forms: spoken (verbal), written (paper), and electronic (digital). The Privacy Rule applies to ALL forms — not just electronic records. This is a key distinction from the Security Rule, which covers only electronic PHI (ePHI).
Covered Entities (CEs) and Business Associates (BAs):
Covered entities (CEs)
Healthcare providers who conduct administrative and financial transactions in electronic form. Includes all employees, volunteers, trainees, and all others under the control of the entity. Examples: hospitals, physician practices, outpatient clinics, health plans, and healthcare clearinghouses. The CE is directly bound by all HIPAA requirements.
Business associates (BAs)
Individuals or organizations that provide functions, activities, or services to a covered entity that involve access to, use of, or disclosure of PHI. Examples: billing companies, EHR vendors, cloud storage providers, transcription services, attorneys handling healthcare matters, accounting firms. BAs must sign a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) and are directly liable for HIPAA violations.
HIPAA Permitted Uses & Disclosures
The Privacy Rule identifies specific categories where PHI may be used or disclosed — either with or without patient authorization. Understanding these permissions is critical for daily healthcare practice.
Disclosures to patients
Patients have the right to access, inspect, and receive copies of their own PHI. Covered entities must provide this access within 30 days of request (one 30-day extension permitted). Cannot be denied except in very limited circumstances.
Use or disclosure for Treatment, Payment, or Health Care Operations (TPO)
Covered entities may share PHI without patient authorization for treatment purposes (sharing with other providers), payment (billing, claims), and healthcare operations (quality improvement, training, peer review). This is the most commonly used permission and covers the vast majority of day-to-day information sharing in healthcare.
Use and disclosure with opportunity to agree or object
Informal permission — the patient is given a clear opportunity to agree or object to a specific use or disclosure. Examples: sharing information with family members involved in the patient's care, including in a hospital directory. If the patient agrees (or fails to object after being given the opportunity), disclosure is permitted.
Incidental uses and disclosures
Disclosures that are secondary to an otherwise permitted use — unavoidable in the course of normal healthcare operations. Permitted as long as reasonable precautions are taken. Examples: another patient overhearing a brief conversation between providers in a hallway; a visitor seeing a patient's name on a whiteboard. Reasonable precautions (lowering voices, positioning screens) are required but perfection is not.
Public interest and benefit activities — 12 national priorities
HIPAA permits PHI disclosure (without patient authorization) for important public purposes including: required reporting (abuse, public health threats, communicable diseases), law enforcement activities, national security and intelligence, organ donation, workers' compensation, research (with appropriate safeguards), and judicial and administrative proceedings. These 12 categories represent carefully balanced exceptions where the public interest outweighs individual privacy.
Limited data set
A partially de-identified data set used primarily for research, public health, and healthcare operations. Specific direct identifiers (name, address, phone, SSN, etc.) are removed, but some geographic and date information may remain. Requires a Data Use Agreement (DUA) between the CE and the recipient — less burdensome than full authorization but still provides some protection.
HIPAA Standard 3 — The Security Rule
The Security Rule applies specifically to electronic PHI (ePHI) — any PHI that is created, received, maintained, or transmitted in electronic form. Unlike the Privacy Rule (which covers all forms of PHI), the Security Rule is limited to electronic records. Its purpose is ensuring the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of ePHI.
Five core Security Rule requirements:
Implement safeguards for ePHI
CEs must implement administrative, physical, and technical safeguards to protect ePHI — covering how data is accessed, stored, and transmitted
Ensure confidentiality, integrity, and availability
Confidentiality = ePHI is not disclosed to unauthorized persons. Integrity = ePHI is not altered or destroyed improperly. Availability = ePHI is accessible to authorized users when needed (disaster recovery)
Protect against anticipated threats
Identify and protect against reasonably anticipated threats to security or integrity — requires ongoing risk assessment to identify vulnerabilities (cyberattacks, natural disasters, equipment failure)
Protect against impermissible uses/disclosures
Protect against reasonably anticipated, impermissible uses or disclosures of ePHI — proactive measures against both external (hackers) and internal (workforce) threats
Ensure workforce compliance
Ensure compliance by their entire workforce — training, policies, sanctions for violations, monitoring, and accountability throughout the organization
Three categories of Security Rule safeguards:
Administrative safeguards
Policies, procedures, workforce training, risk assessments, security officer designation, access management, contingency plans. The most extensive category — covers how the organization manages ePHI security through people and processes.
Physical safeguards
Physical measures to protect electronic systems and equipment. Examples: facility access controls (locked server rooms, key card access), workstation security (screen privacy filters, locking screens when unattended), device and media controls (inventory, proper disposal of hardware containing ePHI).
Technical safeguards
Technology and policies to protect ePHI and control access. Examples: unique user authentication (unique logins for every user), automatic logoff, encryption of ePHI in transmission and at rest, audit controls (logs tracking who accessed what and when), integrity controls (ensuring ePHI hasn't been improperly altered).
Privacy Rule vs. Security Rule — the critical distinction:
Privacy Rule: Covers ALL forms of PHI (spoken, written, electronic). Focuses on who can access, use, and disclose PHI and patients' rights.
Security Rule: Covers ONLY electronic PHI (ePHI). Focuses on technical and administrative safeguards to protect the security of ePHI during transmission and storage.
Breach Notification & HITECH
Breach notification rules:
What is a breach
An impermissible use or disclosure of a patient's record by a covered entity — any access, use, or disclosure that violates the Privacy Rule and that is not low-probability of compromising PHI
Small breaches (<500 records)
Affected individuals must be notified; covered entity must log the breach and report to HHS annually
Large breaches (>500 records)
Must notify: (1) affected individuals, (2) the media in the applicable state or jurisdiction, and (3) HHS without delay. Media notification is required for breaches affecting 500+ records in a single state or jurisdiction.
HHS OCR Breach Database
Since 2009, the HHS Office for Civil Rights (OCR) has maintained a public database of PHI breaches affecting 500+ records — commonly called the "Wall of Shame." Searchable by covered entity, state, and type of breach.
Business associates
Business associates must also follow breach notification guidelines — must notify the covered entity promptly; the CE then notifies affected individuals and HHS
Substantial fines for non-compliance:
HIPAA violations carry a tiered civil penalty structure:
✦ Tier 1 (did not know): $100–$50,000 per violation
✦ Tier 2 (reasonable cause): $1,000–$50,000 per violation
✦ Tier 3 (willful neglect, corrected): $10,000–$50,000 per violation
✦ Tier 4 (willful neglect, not corrected): $50,000 per violation
Annual cap per violation category: $1.9 million
Criminal penalties also apply for intentional violations — up to $250,000 fine and 10 years imprisonment for the most serious violations.
HITECH Act — strengthened protections
The Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health (HITECH) Act (2009) strengthened HIPAA privacy and security protections by:
✦ Making business associates directly liable for HIPAA violations
✦ Requiring breach notification
✦ Increasing civil and criminal penalties
✦ Expanding patient rights (access to electronic records, accounting of disclosures)
✦ Restricting sale of PHI without patient authorization
Fraud & Abuse in Healthcare — Overview
Healthcare fraud costs the U.S. billions of dollars annually. Fraudulent spending is difficult to separate from total healthcare costs, fraud is increasing, and much fraud goes undetected. The Medicare Fraud Strike Force combines federal, state, and local law enforcement agencies to find and prosecute fraud and abuse in federal healthcare programs.
Healthcare fraud
Intentional deception or misrepresentation that an individual knows to be false and makes knowing it could result in some unauthorized benefit to themselves or another person. Fraud requires intent — the person KNEW the claim was false.
Healthcare abuse
Practices that are inconsistent with sound fiscal, business, or medical practices that result in unnecessary cost to healthcare programs. Abuse does NOT require intent — it can result from carelessness, poor practice, or ignorance of proper billing rules.
Four Major Federal Fraud & Abuse Laws
Federal Law 1
Federal False Claims Act (FCA)
Allows individuals to bring civil actions on behalf of the federal government for false or fraudulent claims submitted to federal programs (Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, federal employees' health benefits).

Qui tam provisions: "Qui tam" is a Latin abbreviation for the phrase "he who sues on behalf of the king as well as himself." Under qui tam provisions, a private individual (the "relator" or whistleblower) can file a sealed lawsuit on the government's behalf and share in any recovery. This financial incentive encourages insiders to report fraud.

Whistleblower protections: The FCA protects whistleblowers from retaliation — employees who report fraud cannot be fired, demoted, harassed, or otherwise discriminated against.

Whistleblower reward: Whistleblowers may share 15% to 30% of any court-awarded damages recovered. Because healthcare fraud cases often involve billions of dollars, qui tam settlements can be life-changing for whistleblowers.

Penalties: Treble (triple) damages plus $13,000–$27,000 civil penalty per false claim.
Federal Law 2
Federal Anti-Kickback Law (Anti-Kickback Statute — AKS)
It is illegal to knowingly and willfully receive or pay anything of value to influence a referral of federal healthcare program business. The AKS prohibits any form of remuneration (payment, gift, discount, kickback, bribe, rebate, or any other item of value) exchanged to induce or reward referrals of patients or business covered by Medicare, Medicaid, or other federal programs.

Possible punishments:
✦ Fines — civil monetary penalties up to $100,000 per violation
✦ Prison term — up to 10 years imprisonment per violation
✦ Loss of participation in federal programs — exclusion from Medicare and Medicaid (effectively ending ability to treat elderly and low-income patients in most healthcare settings)

Safe harbors: The government has defined specific "safe harbor" practices that are excluded from AKS liability — including certain employment arrangements, space rentals, personal services arrangements, and group purchasing organizations. Strict compliance with safe harbor requirements is essential.
Federal Law 3
Stark Law (Physician Self-Referral Law)
Physicians or members of their immediate family who have a financial interest in an entity are restricted from referring patients to that entity for designated health services (DHS) covered by Medicare or Medicaid.

Key characteristics:
✦ This is a strict liability law — intent to violate is NOT required. Unlike the AKS (which requires knowing and willful conduct), the Stark Law is violated simply by making a prohibited referral, regardless of the physician's state of mind.
✦ Applies specifically to Medicare and Medicaid programs
✦ "Designated health services" include: clinical laboratory services, physical therapy, radiology, radiation therapy, durable medical equipment, home health services, outpatient prescription drugs, and more
✦ "Financial relationship" includes both ownership interests and compensation arrangements

Penalties: Denial of payment, refund of amounts received, civil monetary penalties up to $15,000 per service, $100,000 for circumvention schemes, and exclusion from federal programs.

Exceptions: Many narrow exceptions exist (in-office ancillary services, group practice arrangements, employment relationships) — compliance is complex and requires careful legal guidance.
Federal Law 4
Criminal Health Care Fraud Statute (18 U.S.C. § 1347)
Prohibits knowingly and willfully executing a scheme — or attempting to execute a scheme — with the intent to:

✦ Defraud any health care program (Medicare, Medicaid, private insurance plans, or any health benefit program) — any plan or contract that provides healthcare benefits
✦ Obtain by false pretenses, representations, or promises any money or property owned by, or under the control of, any health care benefit program

Key elements: Requires both (1) a scheme or attempt to execute a scheme AND (2) knowing and willful intent — this is a criminal statute requiring proof of mental state (mens rea).

Examples of violations: Billing for services not rendered, upcoding (billing for a more expensive service than was provided), unbundling (billing separately for services that should be billed together), billing for medically unnecessary services, falsifying diagnoses to justify treatment or billing.

Penalties: Up to 10 years imprisonment per violation; up to 20 years if serious bodily injury results; up to life imprisonment or death if death results; substantial fines.
Comparing the four laws:
FCA = civil law allowing private parties to sue for false government claims; whistleblower rewards.
AKS = prohibits paying/receiving anything of value for federal program referrals; requires knowing/willful conduct.
Stark Law = prohibits physician self-referrals for designated health services; STRICT LIABILITY (no intent required).
Criminal Fraud Statute = criminal prohibition on schemes to defraud healthcare programs; requires knowing/willful intent; most severe penalties.
